The Issue: A Flood of Corruption?

An analysis of the Flood Control Scandal

The greatest flood ravaging the Philippines isn't because of water, but of corruption. Recent investigations into the country's flood-control projects have revealed a staggering level of this corruption, with an estimated ₱1.089 trillion in climate-related public funds potentially lost since 2023 alone (Greenpeace Philippines, 2025). This massive leakage of the national budget represents a critical and ongoing challenge.

In the Philippine context, corruption is the abuse of entrusted power for private gain, a problem reflected in the country's low score of 34 out of 100 on the 2023 Corruption Perceptions Index (Transparency International, 2024). This systemic issue severely hampers the nation's development by enabling the creation of substandard or non-existent public infrastructure, deterring foreign investment as noted by international observers (U.S. Department of State, 2025), and degrading the essential services that protect and build the country's human capital.

Systemic Corruption & Financial Loss

This systematic corruption severely manipulates the allocation of government funds, particularly in infrastructure and flood control programs.

  • Economic Cost: BusinessWorld (2025) reports that suspected ghost flood control programs in the Philippines are estimated to have cost the economy between ₱42.3 billion and ₱118.5 billion between 2023 and 2025 due to irregularities and non-execution.
  • Definition of Graft: The Philippine Institute for Development Studies elaborates that graft is the siphoning off public funds and abuse of power that erodes public confidence and prevents the provision of basic services (Manhit, 2020).
  • Barangay Level Issues: Carrido (2024) reported that most of the flood control initiatives at the barangay levels were found to be overpriced, abandoned, or built with substandard materials due to theft of funds for personal wants.

And because of this, such ill-spent funds result in ineffective protection against floods, heightened exposure to floods, and slowed economic growth. The misallocation of government funds not just degrades infrastructure but also disables the country from reaching sustainable development.

Loss of Investor Confidence

When government institutions are dominated by systemic corruption, both local and foreign investors lose confidence in the fairness and transparency of business dealings. A striking example of this loss of trust can be seen in South Korea’s withdrawal of its ₱28.7 billion loan plan to the Philippines, a move that reflected concerns over corruption and governance issues, tarnishing the nation’s credibility and discouraging future financial partnerships (Mendoza, 2025).

The Reality of "Ghost Projects"

This loss of international confidence, while diplomatically damaging, is merely a symptom of a far more destructive domestic reality. The "flood control scandal" that erupted between 2024 and 2025 has exposed that the withdrawal of foreign funds is a rational response to a system where infrastructure projects often exist only on paper.

  • COA Investigations: Investigations by the Commission on Audit (COA) have validated these fears, uncovering a proliferation of "ghost projects," which refer to infrastructure that has been fully paid for but is physically non-existent.
  • The Bulacan Case: In Bulacan province alone, state auditors flagged ₱297 million worth of flood control projects where inspectors found no structures at the approved sites, despite documents claiming 100% completion (Daguno-Bersamina, 2025).
  • Fabricated Reports: These are not merely delayed projects; they are deliberate fabrications where funds are siphoned off through falsified accomplishment reports, leaving communities like Barangay San Nicolas in Hagonoy defenseless against rising tides (Baclig, 2025).

Administrative Negligence: The Missing Master Plan

The crisis is compounded by a shocking admission of administrative negligence. Despite the expenditure of hundreds of billions of pesos, the Department of Public Works and Highways (DPWH) admitted during Senate hearings that the Philippines still lacks an integrated, national flood control master plan (Cruz, 2024).

The 5,500 completed projects touted by the administration were revealed to be "piecemeal" solutions, consisting of stand-alone walls or dikes that are not connected to a wider basin-level strategy (Maderazo, 2025). Without a master plan, flood control becomes a localized, politically driven enterprise rather than an engineering necessity. This fragmented approach explains why floodwaters are often simply diverted from one barangay to another, rather than being effectively managed or drained.

The Cost to Agriculture & The Engineering Debate

Impact on Farmers

The consequences of this "infrastructure of plunder" are most visibly suffered by the agricultural sector, which bears the brunt of every policy failure.

  • In regions like Bicol and Central Luzon, the lack of effective water management has created a climate injustice where farmers face a double burden: they are flooded during typhoons and left without irrigation during dry spells.
  • In Sorsogon, farmers' groups have reported that up to 60% of project funds are lost to corruption, resulting in "chopped" or unfinished dikes that fail to protect rice fields during critical harvest periods (Ang Bayan Ngayon, 2025).
  • The ₱350 billion allocated for flood control could have funded massive irrigation networks to support food security; instead, the corruption of these funds leaves the agricultural heartland vulnerable to both drowning and drought (Pangilinan, 2025).

Hard vs. Soft Engineering

Furthermore, the government’s continued reliance on capital-intensive "hard" engineering, such as concrete dikes and unverified dredging projects, runs contrary to modern scientific consensus. Experts from the University of the Philippines Resilience Institute (UPRI) argue that the obsession with concrete is misplaced, as these structures are often designed for outdated flood scenarios and fail catastrophically during extreme weather events like Typhoon Carina (Macababbad, 2025).

Dr. Mahar Lagmay emphasizes that genuine resilience requires "nature-based solutions," such as restoring watersheds and expanding mangrove forests, which are cost-effective and sustainable (Macababbad, 2025). However, these solutions are often ignored by policymakers, likely because they offer fewer opportunities for the large-scale procurement contracts that fuel the kickback economy.

Recommendations & Solutions

Instead of piecemeal projects driven by politics, we propose a complete change of the Philippine's flood control system based on hard data and science.

National Flood Control Transparency Portal

The biggest enabler of the current scandal is the lack of visibility on project progress.

The Fix

Prevent "ghost projects" by mandating a centralized digital portal where every project must have monthly geo-tagged photo updates proving construction progress.

River Basin-Based Budgeting

Budgets are currently "sliced" into small district contracts, leading to uncoordinated, patchwork dikes.

The Fix

Funding should follow the 18 Major River Basins Master Plan. Projects must be integrated across provinces rather than limited by political district boundaries.

Fast-Track High-Impact Projects

Major game-changers like the Parañaque Spillway have been delayed while billions are wasted on small dredging.

The Fix

Prioritize securing ODA (e.g., JICA) for the Parañaque Spillway to drain Laguna de Bay directly to Manila Bay, bypassing the clogged Pasig River.

Citizen Participatory Audits

The Commission on Audit (COA) is understaffed and cannot physically inspect every dike in remote areas.

The Fix

Accredit CSOs and local NGOs as "deputy auditors." Locals must sign a "Certificate of Reality" before final payments are released to contractors.

"Sponge City" Ordinances

Metro Manila is a concrete jungle that prevents rainwater from seeping into the ground.

The Fix

Mandate Nature-Based Solutions (NbS): rainwater cisterns for subdivisions, permeable pavement for parking lots, and wetland restoration.

Decouple Design & Construction

Contractors often influence designs to make them cheaper to build, leading to "politicized engineering."

The Fix

The entity that designs the master plan should not be the one building it. Independent firms must design and supervise to ensure blueprint compliance.

Performance-Based Maintenance

Currently, contractors profit from building the infrastructure, but have no incentive to make it last.

The Fix

Shift to "Pay-for-Performance." Include 5-10 year maintenance clauses where contractors are paid an annual fee only if the area remains flood-free.

Liability for "Enablers"

Waterways are often narrowed by illegal structures permitted by officials for votes or bribes.

The Fix

Impose automatic criminal liability on officials who issue permits within water easements. If a building stands on a creek, the signer goes to jail.

Geo-Tagging Heavy Equipment

"Equipment double-booking" allows contractors to claim the same excavators are working on multiple distant projects at once.

The Fix

Mandate GPS tracking on all government project machinery. The transparency portal must show real-time locations to prevent resource ghosting.

Strict Conflict of Interest Acts

Many officials own the construction companies that win the contracts they vote to fund.

The Fix

Permanently disqualify elected officials and relatives (up to 4th degree) from bidding on projects in their jurisdiction.

Voter Education

Voters often forget specific corruption cases by election time. Politicians rely on this collective amnesia, using the very funds stolen from flood control projects to buy votes.

The Fix

Civil Society Organizations (CSOs) must publish a "Transparency Scorecard" before every election. This scorecard would not just list promises, but specifically tag officials with their involvement in recent scandals, corruption, and allegations.
